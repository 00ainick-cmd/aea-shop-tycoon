# AEA Member Shop Tycoon
## Story Frame and Design Framework

This is the blueprint we design against before we touch the build again. It takes your direction and turns it into one coherent game: a story, a yearly rhythm, a set of systems that feed each other, and an art direction for every screen. Read it, mark it up, answer the questions at the end, and we lock it.

---

## 1. The pitch, in one breath

You take over a small avionics shop on a regional field and build it into the best shop in the country. You do it by choosing what you are great at, earning the dealerships everyone wants, keeping your people trained and your floor safe, and outrunning a rival who is chasing the same prize. The industry never stops moving: every spring a trade show drops new gear, every fall your techs owe their annual training, and every winter the tax bill comes due. Survive the lean years, ride the right waves, and one day the AEA names you Shop of the Year.

## 2. The hero, the guide, and the stakes

The game is built on a simple story spine, the kind that makes a player care.

- **The hero is the player.** Not a chosen one. A shop owner who started with one bay and a dream of doing the work right.
- **The guide is the Chief.** A veteran who has seen every era of this trade and believes you can be great. He shows up at the turning points with a word of wisdom, never to do the work for you.
- **The problem is the industry itself.** It moves faster than any one shop can keep up with. Glass replaces steam, mandates land, new boxes ship every year, and good techs are always being courted away. The rival, Vance Mercer, is the human face of that pressure.
- **The plan is the game.** Specialize. Earn dealerships. Train your people. Run clean. Manage the year.
- **The stakes are real.** Fail and the shop folds. Win and your name goes on the wall, your people become Masters, and the field comes to you.

That spine runs under every screen and every decision.

## 3. The three loops

A good management game nests three loops inside each other. Ours:

| Loop | Length | What the player does | What it feeds |
|---|---|---|---|
| The job loop | A few weeks | Take work, assign crew, run shifts, deliver, get paid | Specialty standing, reputation, cash |
| The year loop | One in-game year | Navigate the four seasons and their fixed events (show, conference, taxes, recruiting) | Dealerships, trained crew, the books |
| The career loop | The whole game | Move through the eras, climb the dealer ladder, chase the top name, win the award | The story, the ending |

The job loop already exists and works. The year loop is the new spine you asked for. The career loop is the arc that gives it all meaning.

## 4. The year: a living calendar

This is the organizing idea you named, and it changes the feel of the game the most. Time moves through four seasons, each with its own work and one fixed event the player has to plan around. The season is always visible, and the art changes with it.

| Season | The mood and the work | The fixed event | The decision it forces |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Winter** | Slow flying, busy benches. Owners bring planes in for annual inspections and upgrades while the weather is bad. | **Tax season.** The year's profit is taxed. | Spend before year end on training or a bay to lower the bill, or bank the cash. |
| **Spring** | The flying season wakes up. Demand climbs. | **The AEA International Convention and Trade Show.** New products are revealed here, and only here. | Pay to attend and unlock the year's new gear and dealer moves, or save the money and fall a year behind. |
| **Summer** | Peak flying, peak demand, fast turns. Owners want their planes back, not parked. | **Recruiting season.** The Part 147 schools have just graduated a new class. | Who do you hire while the talent is fresh, and what do you pay them? |
| **Fall** | The pace eases. Time to invest in your people. | **The AEA regional training conference.** | How many techs do you send, knowing it costs money and pulls them off the floor, but keeps their certifications current? |

The seasons are not just flavor. Each one gates work, prices, and opportunities, so the same shop plays differently in July than in January. The whole point is that the player learns the rhythm of a real avionics business: build cash in the busy season, invest in the slow one, never miss the show, never let training lapse.

## 5. Specialties: what your shop is known for

Right now the shop has one general reputation. That is the flat spot you felt. We replace it with **specialties**, the thing a real shop hangs its name on.

A specialty is a domain of work you can become known for. You raise your standing in a specialty by doing jobs in it, holding the right dealerships, and keeping certified techs on it. Standing climbs through ranks, and rank unlocks the premium work, the better margins, and the respect.

**Proposed specialties (six core, two advanced):**

| Specialty | The work | Anchored dealers |
|---|---|---|
| Retrofit and Panel Upgrades | Tearing out steam gauges, rebuilding panels, the bread and butter | Larkfield, Halcyon |
| Autopilots and Flight Control | Servos, autopilot installs, integration | Tindall |
| Glass and Integrated Decks | PFDs, MFDs, full glass cockpits | Vireon, Meridian |
| Surveillance and Compliance | Transponders, ADS-B, the mandate work | Quanta |
| Engine and Systems Monitoring | Engine monitors, EIS, fuel and systems | Kestrel |
| Cabin and Connectivity | Audio, intercom, datalink, satcom | Larkfield, Stratoline |
| **Business and Turbine** (advanced) | Bizjet and turboprop avionics, the high-dollar work | Stratoline, Meridian |
| **Inspection and Return to Service** (advanced) | The IA authority, the sign-off, the paperwork that closes the job | enabling, not a dealer line |

**Standing ranks (per specialty):** Unrated, Familiar, Proficient, Specialist, Authority. Each rank is a visible badge on your shop board.

The strategic heart of the game lives here: **you cannot be an Authority in everything at once.** Focus is rewarded with faster climbs and fatter margins. Spreading thin is possible but slow. Choosing your lane, and defending it against the rival, is the decision the whole game turns on. This is also a major input to the Shop of the Year award.

## 6. The dealers: a ladder with a king

You want a clear top boss and a roster of fictional brands that wink at the real ones. Here is the ecosystem.

**The flagship (the one everyone wants): Meridian Avionics.** The giant. It makes everything: navigators, integrated decks, transponders, audio, autopilots. Carrying the Meridian badge is the single biggest prestige signal in the game, and it is the prize you and the rival both chase. You do not simply buy it. You earn an invitation by becoming a Premier dealer of at least two other brands, holding a clean multi year safety record, and reaching a high reputation, and then you pay a large buy in on top. Landing the Meridian authorization is the climax of the late game.

**The roster (fictional, each a wink at a real house):**

| Brand | Real world wink | Their lane | Role in the game |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Meridian Avionics** | Garmin | Everything | The king. Invitation plus capital. The endgame prize. |
| **Vireon Avionics** | Avidyne | Integrated decks, touch navigators | The main challenger to Meridian |
| **Halcyon Dynamics** | Dynon | Experimental and certified glass | The disruptor, easy to start with, growing fast |
| **Quanta Avionics** | Trig and uAvionix | Transponders, ADS-B, surveillance | The up and comer that rides the mandate |
| **Tindall Aerosystems** | BendixKing and Genesys | Autopilots and flight control | The legacy house, dependable |
| **Kestrel Instruments** | JPI and EI | Engine and systems monitoring | The specialist's brand |
| **Larkfield Audio** | PS Engineering | Audio panels and intercoms | The easy first dealership, low buy in |
| **Stratoline** | Gogo and SmartSky | Cabin connectivity and datalink | The business and turbine play |
| **Tempest Systems** | weather radar makers | Weather radar and hazard awareness | A high end add later |

Each brand still has the three dealer tiers we built (Stocking, Authorized, Premier), climbed with sales, reputation, and certified techs. The deadlock is already fixed: the first tier never needs a certified tech, so the ladder always has a first rung. Meridian sits above all of it as the locked, invitation only crown.

## 7. New products, revealed at the show

This is one of the most distinctive ideas you brought, and it ties the calendar to the industry's real heartbeat.

Every spring, at the AEA show, the dealers reveal the year's new gear: a new touchscreen navigator, a new integrated flight deck, a new transponder, a new engine monitor. These are fictional analogs of the real milestones (the boxes that changed the industry), named so the wink is clear without using a real product name.

The mechanic:

- New products are **announced only at the show.** If you attend, you unlock the year's gear. If you skip the show to save money, you do not get it, and the shops that did attend ride the wave without you.
- A hot new box creates a **demand wave**: a run of owners who all want it at once, at early adopter prices, before the rest of the field catches up.
- To sell and install a new product you need to be a **dealer of that brand** and have a **trained, certified tech** on it. So the show, the dealer ladder, and the training system all pull on each other.
- The eras drive what gets revealed. Early years reveal the first glass panels; the mandate years reveal the ADS-B boxes; the late years reveal connectivity and the flight decks. The show is where each era's signature technology actually arrives in your shop.

Miss the show and you feel it all year. That is the pressure that makes it worth attending.

## 8. Training, now recurring

You are right that training cannot be one and done. Part 145 means recurring, annual currency for your technicians, and that becomes a real obligation on the calendar.

- Every tech carries an **annual training currency**, a "trained through" status.
- If it lapses, that tech **cannot be assigned to certified or dealer authorized work**, and any dealership that requires trained techs is **suspended** until you refresh. Nothing is lost permanently. You just cannot do the high value work until your people are current again.
- The efficient way to refresh is the **fall conference**, where you send techs in a batch. You can also pay for ad hoc training mid year if you let someone lapse, but it costs more and pulls them off the floor at a worse time.

The training hub now holds four tracks plus the currency tracker:

1. **The credential ladder** (Apprentice, CAET, CAET Advanced, Master). The career progression. Already built.
2. **AEA courses.** One time shop wide investments. Already built.
3. **OEM factory training.** Certify a tech on a line you sell. Already built, dead end now fixed.
4. **The apprenticeship program.** Grow your own, draw grant funding. Already built.
5. **The annual training tracker.** New. Who is current, who is about to lapse, what the fall conference will cover.

## 9. The fall conference, in detail

A real decision every fall, not a popup you click through.

You choose how many techs to send: just yourself, a couple, or the whole crew. The screen shows you the trade clearly:

- **Cost:** tuition and travel, scaled by how many you send.
- **Downtime:** the techs you send are off the floor that week, so those bays sit idle.
- **The payoff:** their annual training currency refreshes, they gain skill and move toward credentials, your AEA standing rises, and there is often a networking perk waiting (a hot lead, a dealer introduction, a recruiting tip).

Send everyone and you are fully current and sharper, but you ate a week of production and a big tuition bill. Send no one and you saved money but you are gambling that nobody lapses. That tension is the game.

## 10. People, pay, and a quieter conscience

The labor market is already in. Every tech has a wage, hiring is a negotiation, loyalty drifts on how you pay and treat them, and a rival will poach the people you underpay. That stays and gets richer as the rival becomes a named, persistent competitor.

**Human factors, redesigned to be subtle.** You have said twice that the safety moments should be quiet and the consequences should be thought out. Here is the redesign:

- **Rare and contextual, never random.** A human factors moment only surfaces when the conditions are real: a fatigued or overworked tech on a complex job, a rushed mandate deadline, an end of quarter push. It reads like a moment on the floor, not a quiz.
- **Mixed immediate and delayed consequences.** A good call quietly builds your safety culture and quietly prevents a hidden defect. A rushed or careless call can plant a latent defect that surfaces later as a callback, an incident, or a finding on an inspection. The delay is the point: it feels like cause and effect over time, not an instant punishment.
- **Legible rewards.** A strong safety culture pays in ways the player can see: lower insurance on the statement, fewer callbacks, smoother inspections, better talent staying and arriving, and eligibility for the safety award and the big dealerships. Safety is an investment with a visible return, not a tax.

## 11. Money and the year

The quarterly profit and loss is already in, with rent, payroll from real wages, insurance that tracks your safety culture, and dealer fees. We add two things to complete the financial year:

- **Taxes.** A winter tax event on the year's profit, with the small business move baked in: spend before year end on training, a bay, or gear to lower the taxable number, or take the profit and pay the tax. Simple, transparent, and thematically true.
- **A line of credit.** A way to borrow against the shop to survive a brutal quarter or fund a big move like a new bay or the Meridian buy in, repaid with interest. This turns the only failure mode from a sudden cliff into a real decision with a recovery path.

## 12. The cast

The people who make it a story, not a spreadsheet.

- **The Chief.** Your guide. Craft and safety made human. Appears at the turning points.
- **The Rook.** The apprentice you take on early, who can grow across the whole game from green kid to Master. A personal through line.
- **Vance Mercer of Mercer Avionics.** The rival shop across the field. Established, good, and chasing the same dealerships and the same techs you are. Not a cartoon villain. A respected competitor in the same AEA community, which makes beating him mean something. The race for the Meridian authorization is your shared obsession.
- **The dealer reps.** The faces of the brands at the show. Meridian's rep is polished and hard to impress. The up and comer's rep is hungry and will give you a shot before anyone else will.
- **The inspector.** The FSDO presence around compliance, safety, and Part 145. Fair but exacting. Run clean and visits are smooth. Run sloppy and they are not.
- **The customers.** Recurring archetypes that give the work a face: the proud Bonanza owner, the flight school with a fleet, the charter operator, the warbird restorer, the business jet flight department. Each leans toward a specialty.

## 13. The arc

Five acts, riding the eras you already built and the years on the new calendar.

- **Act I, The Doors Open.** Steam gauge era, years one and two. Learn the trade, take all work, earn your first specialty rating, hire your first tech, survive your first tax season and first fall conference. Meet the Chief and the Rook. Vance barely knows your name.
- **Act II, Pick Your Lane.** The glass wave begins. Commit to specialties. Land your first dealerships, the easy ones first. Attend your first show and ride a new product wave. Build a crew and make your first real pay decisions. Vance starts bidding against you.
- **Act III, The Mandate Years.** ADS-B era. The whole field needs the work at once. Huge opportunity, huge risk. Your trained crew and your safety culture get tested. Head to head with Mercer for the field's business and its best people.
- **Act IV, The Big Name.** The connected cockpit era. You and Vance both chase the Meridian authorization. It demands everything: specialty mastery, a certified and current crew, a clean record, capital, and the earned invitation. Landing it, before he does, is the climax.
- **Act V, Shop of the Year.** The capstone. The weighted award reads your whole shop, the dealerships, the trained Masters, the safety record, the standing. Your name goes on the wall.

## 15. Art direction: an elevated Sega Genesis

We keep the sixteen bit Genesis soul you love and raise the craft to match. The discipline here is the one rule that matters most: **spend the boldness in one place.** The hangar scene is that place. Everything around it stays quiet, confident, and consistent.

**Palette.** Anchored on your steel, amber, and oscilloscope green, expanded into a disciplined Genesis palette with bold high contrast and honest dithered gradients. The sky and the season banner carry seasonal accents: winter steel blue, spring green, summer gold, fall rust. (Exact hex set to be locked in the build plan.)

**Type.** Press Start 2P for display and headers, used with restraint. VT323 for the green readouts and longer text. A clean condensed face for small labels and data. Strong hierarchy, never a wall of pixel font. The type treatment is part of the identity, not a neutral delivery vehicle.

**The console UI kit.** Beveled and riveted metal panels. Chunky physical buttons with real press states. Oscilloscope green LED readouts. Amber for warnings and prestige. Badge chips for dealers and specialties. A cohesive sixteen bit icon set for specialties, dealers, and seasons. The season banner as a recurring structural device that actually tells you something true, the time of year and what it means, not decoration.

**The hangar hero, the signature.** Parallax depth, animated techs at work, ambient life (drifting clouds, a flickering shop light, a flyover), the second shift amber lighting motif, and a scene that visibly evolves with your level, your era, and your season. This is the one thing the game is remembered by. It is where we are maximalist. We earn it by keeping the rest of the interface precise and calm.

**Motion.** Deliberate, not scattered. A short load reveal, the ambient hangar life, satisfying button presses, the money counting up on a payout. Reduced motion respected.

**Quality floor, never announced.** Responsive down to the phone, forty four pixel touch targets, visible focus, no hover only interactions, page always scrolls. The standing code rules hold: no fixed viewport height caps, flexbox with min height, tested at 1366 by 768, no version numbers in file names, and not a single em dash anywhere.

## 16. What is already built, so we are not starting over

The base is solid and stays. Already in and verified: the core job loop, multiple bays, the twelve aircraft, the four eras, the credential ladder, the dealer ladder with tiers, AEA courses, OEM factory training, the apprenticeship program, recruiting with real wages and negotiation, loyalty with poaching, the quarterly profit and loss with culture driven insurance, the safety culture meter, and the weighted Shop of the Year award.

This framework extends that base. It does not replace it. The new work is the annual calendar spine, specialties as the reputation model, the Meridian crown and the new product reveals, recurring Part 145 training, the spring show and the fall conference as fixed seasonal events, taxes and the line of credit, the quieter human factors, and the art overhaul across every screen.

## 17. The decisions I need from you

I have taken a position on everything so we can move. These are the few where your call as the industry expert changes the design, so I want your read before we build.

1. **The specialties list.** Do the six core plus two advanced match how shops actually specialize? Should Business and Turbine be its own specialty or a tier inside the others? This list is the spine of the reputation system and I want it right.

2. **The dealer roster and the king.** Does the Meridian as Garmin mapping land, and are the other brand names the right level of wink? I recommend we keep the brands and products fictional in game rather than using real trademarks, so the names nod to the real houses without naming them. And I lean toward Meridian being unlocked by an earned invitation plus a large buy in, not pure cash, so it stays the prestige climax. Agree, or do you want it purchasable outright?

3. **The pace of the calendar.** I propose a week as the unit of time, each season a handful of weeks, a full year as the core loop, so the show, the conference, taxes, and recruiting land at a satisfying rhythm. Does a year based loop at that pace feel right, or do you want time to move faster so years pass quicker?

4. **How hard a training lapse bites.** My proposal: a lapsed tech cannot do certified or dealer work and the affected dealership is suspended until you refresh, with nothing lost permanently. Is that the right level of teeth, lighter, or heavier?

5. **You, the owner.** I propose you start hands on, able to work jobs yourself early, then grow into pure management as you hire. The authentic small shop arc. Good, or do you want to be a manager from day one?

Answer those five and tell me anything else that is sitting wrong, and I will lock this framework and start the build with the one thing you most want to see: the redesigned Floor, the hero screen, as a visual proof of concept before we wire the new systems behind it.
