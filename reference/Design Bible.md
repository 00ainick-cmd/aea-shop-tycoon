# AEA Member Shop Tycoon: Bench to Board
### A game design bible and storyboard

**Logline.** Start as a one-bench avionics shop in 1983. Ride forty years of the avionics revolution, from steam gauges to the connected cockpit. Build it clean, grow it into an empire, and end up shaping the very standards and people that keep the whole industry flying.

**Tagline.** One bench. Forty years. The whole industry.

---

## The pitch

Most tycoon games give you bigger numbers. This one gives you a life.

You begin as a technician with grease under your nails and a hand-painted sign going up over a back-room shop. You learn the trade, you win the right work, and you never let a squawk leave the building. Then the industry starts to turn over under your feet. GPS shows up and kills LORAN. Glass replaces steam. A federal mandate sets a clock that every aircraft on the field has to beat. You ride each wave or you get left behind.

And here is the part nobody else builds: you do not just survive the industry. You climb above the bench. You open shops in other towns and hand them to the apprentices you raised. You run for the AEA board. You get elected, and now your decisions ripple across the entire game world, certification standards, workforce funding, how the next mandate lands. The kid who walked in green in 1995 is running their own shop by the time you are casting industry-wide votes.

The game teaches the trade while you play it. It is fun, it is real, and it has a destination that means something.

---

## What makes it award winning

**Authenticity is the feature.** Real avionics history, real shop life, real human factors. Players come out understanding how an avionics shop actually runs and how the industry actually evolved. For a game that wants to showcase and recruit for the trade, that is the whole point.

**The bench-to-board arc.** Your role grows vertically, not just your bank account. You go from soldering one radio to setting the standards everyone follows. That climb is the emotional engine, and it pays off in a way bigger sales numbers never could.

**Mentorship is mechanical.** The apprentices you train graduate into managers, into independent shop owners, into future board members. The game gives you a reason to care about people, and the story cashes that reason in.

**Teaching through play.** Squawk troubleshooting trains real diagnostic logic. The Dirty Dozen moments train real safety culture. The interaction is the teaching. Players learn the craft by doing the craft.

**Integrity compounds.** Doing it right, quality, paperwork, safety, taking care of your crew, pays off over decades inside the actual math. Cutting corners can win a quarter and cost you the career. The game's values live in its systems, not in a tutorial popup.

**A world that remembers.** Customers, rivals, and apprentices persist and change across the eras. The doctor with the Bonanza upgrades through every panel generation. The rival shop booms or busts based on the bets it made. Continuity makes the world feel alive.

---

## The spine: bench to board

Read top to bottom, this is the whole journey.

You open a shop. You learn to win the right work and put the right hands on it. You grow from one bench to a full floor. You bet on the technologies that reshape the panel, and you bet early or you bet safe. You open a second location, then a third, and you staff them with people you trained. Your reputation goes regional, then national. The AEA invites you to run for the board. You campaign on the floor of the convention, you get elected, and you start making the calls that shape the trade itself. At the end you are the old-timer on day one of someone else's career, handing down the same creed that got handed to you.

The shape of the arc: **fix the airplane, run the shop, build the empire, shape the industry, raise the next generation.**

## The storyboard

This is the cinematic and narrative spine, act by act. Each panel is a beat: what is on screen, what happens, and why it matters.

### Cold open. Day one, 1983.

**Panel 1.** Black screen. One sound: a soldering iron hissing, then a radio crackling to life through static. White text fades up and holds. "1983."

**Panel 2.** We pull back slowly off the orange glow of a single component on a circuit board to reveal a cramped back-room bench. A cassette deck. A coffee-stained work order on a clipboard. An ADF head sitting in a vise. Everything lit in warm amber. Pixel art, but it breathes.

**Panel 3.** A weathered hand sets a single key down on the bench. This is Chief. "This is yours now. The lease. The bench. The name on the door." Cut to the shop front from outside as a hand-painted sign goes up over the door.

**Panel 4.** Chief leans in close. He delivers the line the whole game is built on. "Win the right work. Put the right hands on it. And never, ever let a squawk leave this building." This becomes your creed. You will say it to someone else in the final scene.

**Panel 5.** Your hand flips the sign from CLOSED to OPEN. Through the window, a set of headlights swings into the gravel lot. Your first customer. Title card slams in: **[SHOP NAME] EST. 1983.**

*Beat: the thrill and the terror of being on your own. The player owns this from frame one.*

### Act I. The radio shop. Early 1980s.

**Panel 6. First job.** A piston single rolls in with a dead nav radio. You win the work off the board, assign yourself, and build it. The core loop is born.

**Panel 7. First squawk.** On the functional test, a nav flag drops only in turbulence. You chase it, find the chafed wire behind the panel, and the flag clears. The rush of catching it before it shipped. Chief gives the smallest nod. You will chase that nod for years.

**Panel 8. First hand.** You hire your first tech. Two benches now. The shop has a heartbeat.

**Panel 9. The Mode C wave.** Word comes down after the airspace rules tighten. Every aircraft working the busy fields needs a Mode C transponder. The board floods. Your first taste of riding a wave instead of getting hit by one.

**Panel 10. Joining the family.** A flyer lands on the bench for the AEA convention. You become a member. A community of shops just like yours, the people who will one day endorse you. Plant the seed.

*Beat: you are nobody yet, and you are building something real.*

### Act II. The GPS revolution. The 1990s.

**Panel 11. The rumor.** A trade magazine sits on the bench. The cover says GPS is coming to the cockpit. Chief scoffs at "computers." You feel the ground shifting.

**Panel 12. The bet.** A strategic fork the player will remember. Invest now, get the training, chase the dealer authorization, retool the shop for GPS. Or stay safe with the LORAN and radio work you know. The early movers in real life won big. The cautious got passed.

**Panel 13. The rival's choice.** Across the field, Vance at the flashier shop makes the opposite bet from you. His fate will track that choice for the rest of the game. A foil, a mirror, a warning.

**Panel 14. Rook walks in.** A green kid in a borrowed shirt asks for a shot. Low hours, eager, hungry. You hire the apprentice. This is the most important person in the story besides Chief, and the player does not know it yet.

**Panel 15. The 430 lands.** The watershed box arrives. GPS, nav, and com in one unit with a moving map. The panel changes forever. LORAN customers line up to convert. If you bet early, you are slammed and rich. If you bet safe, you are scrambling to catch up.

*Beat: standing at the crossroads of an industry turning over, and your call mattered.*

### Act III. Glass and WAAS. The 2000s.

**Panel 16. The wave breaks.** WAAS goes live and suddenly GPS can fly approaches down to near-ILS minimums. Glass cockpits hit general aviation. The steam-to-glass retrofit gold rush begins.

**Panel 17. The big one.** Your first full glass retrofit on a high-value airframe. Steam gauges out, integrated glass in. High pay, high skill, credential-gated. The kind of job that makes a shop's name. Everyone holds their breath on the functional test.

**Panel 18. The second sign.** You open a satellite shop in another town. A new hand-painted sign over a new door, in a new region with its own aircraft and its own demand. The empire begins. You cannot be at both benches, so you have to trust people.

**Panel 19. Rook earns it.** A quiet ceremony beat. The kid you hired in 1995 earns CAET. You pin it on. The legacy system just became visible. The student is becoming the craftsman.

**Panel 20. Paying it forward.** You sponsor your first scholarship through the AEA Educational Foundation. Somewhere, a future apprentice gets a shot because you did. The mission shows up for the first time, and it will be the endgame.

*Beat: you went from surviving the field to leading it.*

### Act IV. The mandate. The 2010s.

**Panel 21. The clock.** The FAA finalizes the ADS-B rule. A countdown clock appears on the shop floor and does not leave. "ADS-B OUT. JANUARY 1, 2020." Every aircraft in the controlled airspace has to beat it.

**Panel 22. The gold rush.** The single biggest boom in general aviation history. Bays full across every shop you own. Hiring sprees. Capacity planning. The real risk now is overextending and snapping. You are managing an empire against a deadline.

**Panel 23. The rival, decided.** Vance's bet from the 90s finally pays off or finally kills him. His shop booms into a regional power or busts and goes dark. The world remembered.

**Panel 24. Chief's last day.** The gut punch. Chief hangs up his tools. On his way out he sets his old torque wrench on your bench, the way he set that key down in 1983. "You did right by the trade. Keep it that way." The torch is passed. Hold the frame.

**Panel 25. The invitation.** A letter from the AEA. Your name has come up. They want you to run for the board. The meta-aspiration crystallizes. The bench is no longer the ceiling.

*Beat: the peak of the trade, and the pull toward something bigger than any one shop.*

### Act V. The connected cockpit and the board. The 2020s. Present day.

**Panel 26. The new frontier.** Post-mandate. Cabin connectivity, datalink, touchscreen decks, emergency autoland, cybersecurity for connected aircraft. The work is software as much as wire now.

**Panel 27. The campaign.** A full mini-game on the floor of the convention. You build a platform, workforce, standards, member benefits. You win endorsements from every shop you ever helped. You take the debate stage against a rival candidate. Your past choices, did you mentor, did you cut corners, did you show up, decide your support.

**Panel 28. Election night.** The room at the convention. The count comes in. You win a seat on the AEA board. The kid with the soldering iron is now a voice for the entire industry.

**Panel 29. The boardroom.** Your first industry-wide vote. The topic on the table is certification standards, the real CAET. Your decision ripples across the whole game world. A strong standards push raises the talent pool, which means better hires for every shop, including yours. Systemic feedback you can feel.

**Panel 30. The crisis.** The final boss is not a competitor. It is the workforce shortage. Not enough trained techs for the work the industry needs done. Your answer is the thing that has been building since Act III: certification, apprenticeships, scholarships, the Foundation. You fund the next generation into existence.

**Panel 31. The legacy reveal.** Pull back across the field. Rook runs their own shop now, sign and all. Other apprentices you raised run locations or sit on boards. The people outlived the panels.

**Panel 32. The cycle closes.** A back-room shop. Warm amber light. A green kid in a borrowed shirt on day one. You, now the old-timer, set a key on the bench and lean in. "Win the right work. Put the right hands on it. And never, ever let a squawk leave this building." Smash to title.

*Beat: you became the Chief for the next generation. The creed that started the game ends it, in your voice now.*

## Characters

**You.** The player. Name your tech, name your shop, paint your sign. You age across forty years. Your hands are the through-line.

**Chief.** The mentor and foreman who teaches you the trade and gives you the creed. He is skeptical of every new technology and right about every fundamental. His retirement in Act IV is the emotional hinge of the game. His values become your strategy.

**Rook.** The green apprentice you hire in the 90s. Over the decades you grow them from raw hire to CAET to Master. They graduate into a manager, an independent owner, or a fellow board member, depending on how you play. The legacy made flesh. By the end, Rook is doing for someone else what you did for them.

**Vance, the rival.** Another shop owner across the field. He chases trends and cuts corners, the mirror image of your integrity. His bets in each era pay off or sink him based on the world sim. He poaches your people, undercuts your prices, and gives you someone to beat. His fate is a running commentary on the choices the player did not make.

**The recurring customers.** A small cast of owners whose stories develop across the eras. The doctor with the Bonanza who upgrades through every panel generation. The flying club that signs a contract and keeps coming back. The charter operator who grows from one jet into a fleet. They make the world continuous.

**The AEA.** Not just a logo. The community of shops in Act I, the convention you keep returning to, and the board you aspire to. The institution that develops the workforce and sets the standards, which is exactly what the endgame is about.

## Meta systems

The "open more shops, run for the board, all that good stuff."

### The empire: multiple shops

Once you are established in Act III, you unlock satellite shops in other regions. Each region has its own aircraft mix and its own demand. Coastal jet money. Heartland GA volume. Mountain turboprop work. You assign managers, and the best managers are the apprentices you raised. Each shop runs semi-autonomously while you set priorities, allocate capital, and parachute in when a crisis hits. Reputation becomes both regional and national. The late game lifts you off any single bench and into running a company.

### The AEA board: campaign and governance

This is the prestige endgame.

**Eligibility** is earned, not bought. Years in the trade. AEA membership tenure. Scholarships funded. Apprentices graduated. Reputation across regions. You qualify by living the values, not by grinding cash.

**The campaign** is a mini-game on the convention floor. You set a platform on the issues that matter, workforce, standards, member benefits, advocacy. You collect endorsements from the shops you helped over the years. You take a debate stage against a rival candidate. Your entire history is your campaign. Mentored people and ran a clean shop? You have a base. Cut corners and poached talent? It comes back on you.

**Governance** is the payoff. Once elected, you face periodic board decisions, industry-wide votes on certification standards, responses to FAA mandates, workforce initiatives, advocacy positions. These choices change the whole world. Push certification hard and the talent pool rises, so every shop, including yours, hires better. Fund scholarships and future apprentices appear. Shape how a mandate rolls out and you change the boom for everyone. The bench taught you to fix one airplane. The board lets you fix the conditions every airplane is fixed under.

### Legacy and New Game Plus: the next generation

Apprentices live on a legacy tree. They graduate into your managers, into independent shops that become allies or rivals, or into board members who sit beside you. After a full career you can pass your shop to a successor and start again as that successor or as your own kid, in a slightly later era, carrying legacy bonuses forward. Replayability with continuity. The story never really ends, it gets handed down.

### The living industry

Prices, demand, mandates, and rival shops evolve era to era with real randomness layered on the historical spine. The GPS adoption curve, the ADS-B rush, the glass wave, all create boom and bust cycles you have to read and time. No two careers play out the same, but every career moves through the same real history.

## How integrity compounds

This is the system that makes the game's values real instead of preached.

Quality, clean paperwork, safe practices, and taking care of your crew all feed long-term reputation, repeat customers, board eligibility, and retention. Cutting corners can spike a quarter, but it seeds warranty callbacks, failed audits, lost reputation, poached and bitter crew, and a campaign that collapses when your record gets read out loud. The Dirty Dozen moments are where this lives turn to turn. The career arc is where it pays off. Doing it right is not the moral of the game. It is the optimal strategy, and the player discovers that by playing.

## What we build first

You said "to start," so here is the path from this page to the game.

The core loop is already built and proven: win work, assign crew, build the four quality dimensions, chase the squawk, deliver, grow. The multi-bay, human factors, and AEA credential systems are in. That is the Act I and Act II engine, working today.

From here, in order of impact:

First, the **narrative layer**. Chief as a real character with the cold open, the era beats, and the retirement. Named persistent customers. The creed bookend. This is the cheapest, highest-impact thing we can add, and it is what you want to lean into.

Second, the **era march as story**, not just unlocks. Each act transition becomes a scripted historical moment with its strategic fork, GPS, glass, the mandate clock.

Third, the **empire layer**. Second and third shops with managers drawn from your apprentices.

Fourth, the **board endgame**. Eligibility, the campaign mini-game, and governance votes that ripple across the world.

Fifth, **legacy and New Game Plus**. The apprentice tree and the hand-down.

We build the saga the way the saga is lived. One era at a time.
